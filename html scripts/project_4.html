Polysubstance Use & Mental Health

Exploring how patterns of multiple substance use relate to perceived mental health

Why This Study Matters

Mental health is one of the most pressing public health concerns today, yet the relationship between substance use and mental well-being is complex. While many studies focus on single substances, real-world behavior often involves the use of multiple substances.

This project investigates whether polysubstance use is associated with differences in perceived mental health, using a large-scale national survey dataset. The findings can help inform targeted interventions and evidence-based policy decisions.

Dataset

The analysis is based on the Canadian Tobacco and Nicotine Survey 2022, collected by Statistics Canada.

  • Type: National survey data
  • Observations: 12,133 individuals
  • Features: 88 variables
  • Key domains: Substance use, demographics, perceived mental health

Study Aims

  • Cluster individuals based on substance use patterns and demographic characteristics
  • Test the association between identified clusters and perceived mental health

Methodology

1. Data Cleaning

Survey responses such as “valid skip” and “not stated” were treated as distinct categories to preserve information rather than removing records.

2. Feature Engineering

  • Removed redundant or unrelated variables
  • Retained core substance use variables: alcohol, cannabis, smoking, and vaping
  • Included demographic features such as age and gender
  • Excluded variables that could introduce data leakage

3. Unsupervised Modelling

Two clustering techniques were applied to uncover latent patterns in the data:

  • K-Modes Clustering — optimal clusters selected using the Elbow Method and Silhouette Score
  • Bayesian Gaussian Mixture — optimal number of classes determined using Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) and Silhouette Score

Models were trained on two datasets:

  • Substance use variables only
  • Substance use variables combined with demographics

Cluster interpretability was enhanced using Multiple Correspondence Analysis (MCA) for visualization.

Association Testing

To evaluate whether the identified clusters were associated with perceived mental health, statistical testing was conducted:

  • Chi-Square Test to assess statistical significance
  • Cramér’s V to measure the strength of association

Key Results

  • All clustering approaches showed statistically significant associations (p < 0.05)
  • Cramér’s V values ranged from ~0.08 to ~0.10, indicating weak but meaningful associations
  • Including demographic variables altered cluster structure but did not eliminate associations
  • Bayesian Gaussian Mixture models showed slightly stronger associations overall

Interpretation

While the measured associations were modest, this is expected in public health and social science research, where outcomes like mental health are influenced by many interacting factors.

Even weak associations can be highly relevant when working with large populations and complex behavioral data, reinforcing the value of clustering-based approaches for exploratory analysis.

Conclusion

This study demonstrates that polysubstance use patterns are significantly associated with perceived mental health outcomes. By combining unsupervised learning with statistical testing, the analysis provides a nuanced understanding of how substance use behaviors relate to mental well-being.

From a data analytics perspective, this project highlights the practical application of clustering, dimensionality reduction, and association testing on large-scale survey data to generate actionable insights.
